# PI 9 — Prof. Daisuke Matsubara

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Diagnostic Pathology   
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba     
**Research Model:** Surgical lung cancer specimens, SCLC & LCNEC tissue samples, cancer cell lines (lung adenocarcinoma, SCLC, esophageal carcinoma)   
**Research Theme:** What is YAP1-positive pulmonary neuroendocrine carcinoma?   

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **YAP1-positive pulmonary neuroendocrine carcinoma**, focusing on:

* **Analysis of surgical specimens and cell lines** (41 lung adenocarcinoma, 14 SCLC, 10 esophageal carcinoma cell lines) to explore molecular targets based on markers and morphology.
* **Spatial and genomic profiling** using Visium analysis, whole genome analysis, and single-cell analysis of resected lung cancers.
* **Mechanisms of abnormal differentiation** (dedifferentiation, neuroendocrine differentiation, EMT, gastrointestinal epithelial differentiation).
* **Drug sensitivity and resistance mechanisms** using cancer cell line models.

**Key molecular regulator:** **YAP1**, a central effector of the Hippo pathway associated with non-neuroendocrine phenotype and chemoresistance.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: YAP1-positive Pulmonary Neuroendocrine Carcinoma

Main types of lung cancer (2021 WHO classification):

1. Adenocarcinoma
2. Squamous cell carcinoma (SqCC)
3. Neuroendocrine tumors

   * Neuroendocrine carcinoma

     * Small cell lung carcinoma (SCLC)
     * Large cell neuroendocrine carcinoma (LCNEC)
   * Low-grade neuroendocrine tumors

     * Typical carcinoid
     * Atypical carcinoid
4. Large cell carcinoma

High-grade neuroendocrine carcinomas (HGNEC):
**SCLC and LCNEC**

**Key Insight:** YAP1-positive cases challenge the classical neuroendocrine classification.

---

#### 2. Small Cell Lung Carcinoma (SCLC)

**Clinical Characteristics:**

* Strongly correlated with smoking
* Usually central location
* Highly invasive and metastatic
* Sensitive to chemotherapy and radiation (initially)
* ~10% five-year survival rate
* Surgery rarely feasible (diagnosed at advanced stage)

**Morphology:**

* Small cells
* Scant cytoplasm
* Finely granular (“salt and pepper”) chromatin
* Inconspicuous nucleoli

**Neuroendocrine Markers:**

* CD56
* Chromogranin A
* Synaptophysin
* INSM1

---

#### 3. Molecular Subtyping of SCLC

Classification proposed by **Charles Rudin** (Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center):

Four transcriptional regulators:

* ASCL1
* NEUROD1
* POU2F3
* YAP1

**Roles:**

* ASCL1 / NEUROD1 → regulate neuroendocrine differentiation
* POU2F3 → master regulator of chemosensory cells
* YAP1 → Hippo pathway effector, linked to proliferation and drug resistance

**Important Finding:**

About 18% (9/51) of SCLC cell lines showed high YAP1 expression.

YAP1 and neuroendocrine markers are **inversely correlated** in high-grade neuroendocrine carcinomas.

Pearsall et al. (2020):
CTC-derived tumors contained small fractions of YAP1-positive / NE-negative cells.

---

#### 4. Classic vs Variant SCLC

Described historically by **Adrian Gazdar** and colleagues:

* Classic → floating, neuroendocrine phenotype
* Variant → adherent, non-neuroendocrine phenotype

Experimental models (e.g., Calbo et al.) showed:

* Floating NE phenotype
* Adhesive EMT phenotype

Subtype switching observed:

* MYC activation → NOTCH signaling
* Transient reprogramming from ASCL1-positive to YAP1-positive cells
* Chemotherapy resistance may induce switch toward inflamed/YAP1-like phenotype

**Key Insight:** SCLC is highly plastic and capable of subtype switching.

---

#### 5. Large Cell Neuroendocrine Carcinoma (LCNEC)

Proposed in 1991 by **William D. Travis** as a distinct neuroendocrine carcinoma.

WHO classification groups LCNEC and SCLC as **high-grade neuroendocrine tumors (HGNET).**

Challenges:

* Without immunostaining, LCNEC may be misclassified as adenocarcinoma
* 3–4% of lung adenocarcinomas express NE markers

George classification:

* Type I → STK11/KEAP1 mutation, NE high, NOTCH low
* Type II → RB1 mutation, NE low, NOTCH high

---

#### 6. Prognostic and Therapeutic Implications

* YAP1-positive HGNECs are more chemoresistant
* YAP1-negative HGNECs are more chemotherapy-sensitive
* Many LCNECs contain mixed YAP1-positive / NE-negative populations
* YAP1 immunostaining may help predict chemotherapy response

**Key Insight:** YAP1 expression correlates with chemoresistance and may guide treatment decisions.

---

### Summary Insight

* SCLC-Y is mainly a concept derived from cell lines, not primary tumors.
* SCLC is heterogeneous — most cells are NE-positive and YAP1-negative, but minor YAP1-positive populations exist.
* LCNEC frequently shows mixed YAP1/NE expression patterns.
* YAP1 may serve as a predictive biomarker for chemotherapy response in high-grade neuroendocrine carcinomas.

# PI 10 — Prof. Masafumi Muratani

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Genome Biology     
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba      
**Research Model:** Humans and mice (including spaceflight studies)     
**Research Theme:** Omics Analysis in Space Life Sciences       

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory develops **genomics technologies for limited sample analysis** and applies them to a broad range of projects, including:

* **Spaceflight biology**: analyzing physiological and molecular changes in humans and mice aboard the International Space Station (ISS)
* **Multi-omics analysis**: genome, epigenome, transcriptome (RNA-seq, cfRNA-seq, small RNA-seq, cfDNA, MeDIP) of biobanked clinical samples
* **Automation of laboratory processes**: using the Labdroid “Maholo” to replicate human pipetting and optimize cell culture
* **Research platform management**: coordinating Tsukuba i-Laboratory to provide RNA-seq, ChIP-seq, exome, and gene panel analysis for internal and external users

**Key molecular regulators / pathways:** Gravity-responsive genes, oxidative stress pathways, mitochondrial RNA and DNA, circadian rhythm regulators.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Spaceflight-Induced Physiological and Molecular Changes

* Spaceflight causes systemic changes in humans and mice, including:

  * Muscle atrophy and bone density loss
  * Altered immune and cardiovascular function
  * Circadian rhythm disruption
  * Oxidative stress and metabolic shifts

* Control conditions for microgravity effects:

  * Onboard 1G centrifuge controls (e.g., **MARS – Multiple Artificial-gravity Research System**)
  * Distinguishes gravity effects from radiation and confinement

**Key Insight:** Multi-omics profiling allows integration of physiological and molecular responses to space environmental stressors.

---

#### 2. Plasma Cell-Free Epigenome and Transcriptome Analysis

**Techniques used:**

* Plasma sampling from 6 astronauts × 11 time points = 66 samples
* RNA-seq: cfRNA (mRNA, long RNA), small RNA (miRNA), cf-mitochondrial RNA
* DNA-seq: sonicated plasma DNA, cfDNA size selection
* Epigenetic profiling: MeDIP (methylation DNA immunoprecipitation)

**Observations:**

* 286 genes identified with significant changes (ANOVA FDR < 0.05, ≥2× difference)
* In-flight molecular changes were often slower than post-flight recovery
* Tissue-specific cfRNA alterations detected (e.g., cerebellum-related genes)

**Key Insight:** Spaceflight induces widespread, tissue-specific transcriptomic and epigenomic changes.

---

#### 3. Mouse Flight Experiments

**Observed phenotypes:**

* Muscle fiber type changes and atrophy
* Bone density loss
* Thymus mass alteration
* Oxidative stress and metabolic shifts

**Analysis:**

* RNA-sequencing of multiple tissues to detect altered tissue-specific gene expression
* Pathway analysis with DAVID tool for gravity- and radiation-responsive genes

**Key Insight:** Spaceflight effects are not purely mechanical; multi-omics reveals underlying molecular drivers.

---

#### 4. Laboratory Automation and Robotic Biology

* **Labdroid “Maholo”** mimics human pipetting and performs complex cell culture tasks
* 15 joints and two arms replicate full range of human motion
* Used for:

  * Optimization of regenerative medicine cell cultures
  * Systematic search for optimal experimental conditions

**Experimental Insight:**

* Automation enables reproducible, high-throughput analyses of limited or precious samples
* Reduces variability from manual handling

---

#### 5. Multi-Omics Integration and Data Analysis

* Comparative analysis between astronaut plasma and mouse spaceflight data
* Integration of RNA-seq, small RNA-seq, cfDNA-seq, and MeDIP
* PCA and ANOVA used to identify significant changes
* Highlights inter-individual baseline variation and temporal dynamics

**Key Insight:** Multi-omics provides a comprehensive view of space-induced molecular alterations, capturing both baseline variability and dynamic responses.

---

#### 6. Biological Implications and Evolutionary Perspective

* Spaceflight uncovers **naïve phenotypes** when gravity effects are removed
* Observed phenotypes relate to vertebrate adaptation:

  * Bone and muscle evolution for locomotion
  * Mineral homeostasis and immune system function
  * Circadian rhythm tied to environmental cues
* Provides insights into **robustness and flexibility of biological systems** under extreme environmental stress

**Key Insight:** Understanding spaceflight-induced changes informs human health, regenerative medicine, and evolutionary biology.

---

### Summary Insight

* Spaceflight induces multi-system physiological and molecular changes detectable through **multi-omics technologies**
* Cell-free RNA and DNA, small RNA, and MeDIP reveal tissue-specific and mitochondrial responses
* Automation via Labdroid “Maholo” enhances reproducibility for limited-sample experiments
* Integration of human and mouse data elucidates mechanisms of adaptation, robustness, and potential interventions for spaceflight health risks

# PI 11 — Prof. Satoru Takahashi

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Anatomy and Embryology   
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba   
**Research Model:** Genetically manipulated mice (transgenic, KO, CRISPR-Cas9)   
**Research Theme:** Histological and functional analysis of genetically modified mice to study organ development, transcription factor function and disease modeling.   

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **molecular and cellular mechanisms underlying organ development and disease**, focusing on:

* **Functional analysis of large Maf transcription factor family (MafA, MafB, c-Maf) in organ and cell development**
* **Generation of human disease model mice using CRISPR-Cas9 and gene knockout approaches**
* **Transcriptome analysis of mice in microgravity and artificial 1G conditions**
* **Elucidating biological roles of carbohydrates using glycosyltransferase conditional KO mice**

**Key molecular regulators / pathways / systems** include **Maf transcription factors, GATA-1, autophagy-related pathways, and carbohydrate-modifying enzymes**, studied as central mechanisms for organogenesis, immune function, and disease modeling.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Functional Analysis of Transcription Factors

* Large Maf transcription factors (MafA, MafB, c-Maf) are **transcriptional activators** important for development of pancreatic β-cells, macrophages, podocytes, lens, parathyroid, and other tissues.
* Mutations in these factors can cause **human diseases**, e.g., MafB mutation → Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) with Duane Retraction Syndrome.

**Major steps / components:**

1. Generation of **genetically modified mice** via fertilized egg microinjection and CRISPR-Cas9.
2. Verification of genotype in mice (genotype copy mice).
3. Phenotype characterization (organ, cellular, histological analysis).
4. Functional analysis in disease modeling.
5. Transcriptome profiling to assess gene regulatory impact.

**Key Insight:** Large Maf transcription factors control organ-specific development; their mutations recapitulate human disease phenotypes in mice.

---

#### 2. CRISPR-Cas9 System for Mouse Genome Editing

**Components:**

* **Guide RNA (gRNA):** Recognizes and binds specific genomic sequences.
* **Cas9 protein:** Introduces double-strand breaks at targeted loci.

**Applications in lab:**

* Generation of mice with **MafB DNA-binding domain mutation**, reproducing human FSGS phenotype.
* Establishment of **>2000 genetically modified mice** at the Animal Resource Center.

**Key Insight:** CRISPR-Cas9 allows precise modeling of human mutations in mice, enabling functional and disease studies.

---

#### 3. Transcriptome Analysis in Space Biology

**Experimental Setup:**

* Japan’s first mouse space experiment using **JAXA Mouse Habitat Unit (MHU-1)** on the ISS.
* Conditions: Microgravity (µG) vs. artificial 1G centrifuge onboard.
* Observed **muscle atrophy prevention under 1G artificial gravity**.
* Gene expression profiling performed using **immunohistochemistry and transcriptome sequencing**.

**Key Observation:** Microgravity induces muscle fiber type transition to type IIb, bone density loss, and other tissue-specific transcriptional changes.

---

#### 4. Disease Modeling and Functional Studies

* Investigated mutations in MafB and their impact on renal disease (FSGS) and other developmental defects.
* Demonstrated causality by introducing human mutations into mice using CRISPR-Cas9.
* Observed **homozygous MafB mutation mice** replicate human phenotypes, including early lethality.

**Experimental Insight:**

* Human disease phenotypes can be faithfully modeled in mice.
* Large Maf transcription factors serve as **master regulators of organ-specific differentiation and function**.

---

#### 5. Additional Research Themes

* Functional analysis of **c-Maf**: lens fiber development, IL-4 transcription in T cells, osteoblast differentiation, rhombomere segmentation.
* Study of **glycosyltransferase conditional KO mice** to elucidate carbohydrate functions.
* Analysis of autophagy pathways in development and disease.

---

### Summary Insight

* **Large Maf transcription factors** are essential for organ development and homeostasis; mutations cause severe disease phenotypes.
* **CRISPR-Cas9 mediated genome editing** enables generation of disease model mice and functional studies.
* **Space biology studies** reveal tissue-specific transcriptional adaptations to microgravity.
* Combination of genetic, histological, and transcriptomic analyses provides **integrated insights into development, disease, and environmental responses**.

# PI 12 — Prof. Takatsugu Hirokawa

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Chemical Biology and In Silico Drug Discovery   
**Institute:** Unversity of Tsukuba    
**Research Model:** Computational models, ligand-target complexes, in silico screening pipelines    
**Research Theme:** Introduction to In Silico Drug Discovery.    

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **computational and chemical approaches to drug discovery**, focusing on:

* **Structure-Based Drug Design (SBDD)** using 3D target structures, cryo-EM, and AlphaFold predictions.
* **Ligand-Based Drug Design (LBDD)** guided by compound libraries, fingerprints, and pharmacophore models.
* **Molecular simulations** for predicting ligand-protein interactions, including docking, post-docking refinement, and metadynamics.
* **Integration of AI and deep learning** for improved docking scoring, receptor identification, and de novo compound design.

**Key molecular regulators / pathways / systems** include **GPCRs (e.g., S1PR1), enzymes, ion channels, and receptor-ligand systems**, studied as central mechanisms to understand binding, selectivity, and therapeutic potential.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: In Silico Drug Discovery

* Combines **bioinformatics and chemoinformatics** to streamline identification of drug targets and candidate compounds.
* Addresses unmet therapeutic needs in diseases such as **Alzheimer’s and rare disorders**.

**Major steps / components:**

1. Target identification using **structural genomics** and genomic drug discovery.
2. Ligand- or structure-based compound screening (LBDD/SBDD).
3. Molecular docking and post-docking **energy refinement (MM/PBSA, MD simulations)**.
4. Validation of ligand binding mechanisms using **metadynamics and PLIF analysis**.
5. AI-driven prediction of ligand poses, receptor selection, and de novo compound design.

**Key Insight:** In silico drug discovery accelerates development while reducing costs, enabling focus on previously neglected diseases.

---

#### 2. Ligand-Based Drug Design (LBDD)

**Core Principles:**

* Uses chemical fingerprints and pharmacophore models to represent molecular features.
* **Tanimoto coefficient** is commonly used to compare molecular similarity.
* Lower computational cost than SBDD, effective when protein structures are unknown.

**Key Insight:** LBDD allows efficient screening of vast chemical space (>10^60 molecules) using known active ligands and similarity metrics.

---

#### 3. Structure-Based Drug Design (SBDD)

**Mechanisms / Methods:**

* Docking ligands into 3D protein structures and scoring their binding.
* Post-docking refinement to account for solvation, flexibility, and induced-fit effects.
* Integration of **Cryo-EM, AlphaFold2/3** for high-accuracy protein structures.

**Example:**

* Study of **S1PR1 antagonists (KSI-6666)** showed **Met124 residue critical** for pseudoirreversible inhibition.
* Metadynamics simulations revealed metastable ligand binding states stabilizing long-term inhibition.

---

#### 4. Molecular Simulation & Scoring

* **Docking = search + scoring**: Search explores conformations, scoring approximates binding free energy.
* **MM/PBSA and molecular mechanics** balance computational cost and accuracy.
* AI-based CNN scoring improves pose prediction accuracy compared to traditional scoring functions.

**Experimental Insight:**

* Mutation of Met124 → Val abolished KSI-6666 persistent inhibition, confirming structure-function predictions.
* Demonstrates value of simulations combined with experimental validation for drug mechanism elucidation.

---

#### 5. AI and Deep Learning Integration

* **AlphaFold2/3** predicts protein and nucleic acid structures from sequence data.
* **AlphaFold-Multimer** allows ligand-receptor predictions for single-pass transmembrane proteins.
* **AI-driven de novo compound design** uses reinforcement learning on SMILES representations to propose bioactive molecules.

**Key Insight:** Integration of AI enables prediction of molecular interactions and identification of novel drug candidates that would be impossible with traditional methods alone.

---

#### 6. Future Directions

* Expansion of in silico drug discovery for **ligand optimization and target validation**.
* Integration with **big data and public compound activity databases**.
* Mechanistic studies of **drug resistance, selectivity, and pharmacophore evolution**.

**Key Insight:** Computational pipelines are central to next-generation drug discovery, offering both mechanistic insights and accelerated identification of therapeutic molecules.

---

### Summary Insight

* In silico drug discovery combines **structure-based and ligand-based approaches** to efficiently explore chemical space.
* Molecular simulations, AI, and deep learning **enhance accuracy and predictivity** of drug-target interactions.
* The lab’s work exemplifies **integration of computational and experimental approaches** for rational drug design.
* Broad applicability across **rare disorders, neurological diseases, and immunomodulation therapies**.

# PI 13 — Prof. Kaspar Vogt

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** IIIS G/V Lab     
**Institute:** Unversity of Tsukuba      
**Research Model:** Rodent cortical neurons, tetrode recordings, optogenetic stimulation 
**Research Theme:** Sleep research, neural activity during slow-wave sleep, and memory consolidation   

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **neuronal and network dynamics during sleep**, focusing on:

* **Characterizing cortical activity patterns** in non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep.
* **Understanding synaptic mechanisms of memory consolidation** during sleep.
* **Investigating homeostatic and replay-based processes** underlying sleep-dependent plasticity.
* **Applying electrophysiology and optogenetics** to study single-neuron and population-level activity.

**Key molecular regulators / pathways / systems** include **synaptic zinc signaling, Homer1a, mGluR1/5 pathways, and mossy fiber synapses**, which are studied to understand their role in sleep regulation and memory formation.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Sleep as an Active Process

* Sleep is essential for cognitive functions, not merely passive rest.
* Slow-wave sleep (SWS) features alternating silent OFF and active ON states in cortical neurons.

**Major steps / components:**

1. Waking-to-NREM transition: neurons become more synchronized.
2. Alternation between active (ON) and silent (OFF) periods generates EEG slow waves.
3. High-activity neurons during wake decrease activity; low-activity neurons increase.
4. Spike-pattern predictability (entropy) increases in NREM sleep.
5. Cortical responses to inputs are largest in NREM, intermediate in REM, and muted in waking.

**Key Insight:** Sleep-specific cortical patterns support unique cognitive processes and memory consolidation.

---

#### 2. Sleep and Memory Consolidation

**Subheading: Replay**

* Reactivation of waking activity patterns during sleep strengthens memory performance.
* Replay occurs in specific engram networks and is temporally precise.

**Subheading: Synaptic Homeostasis (SHY)**

* Global process where synapses are downscaled to maintain balance.
* Weakens overall synapses while maintaining key network information.

**Key Insight:** Memory consolidation during sleep involves interplay between global homeostatic scaling and selective replay.

---

#### 3. Synaptic Mechanisms

**Mossy Fiber Synapses and Zinc Signaling:**

* Mossy fibers co-release glutamate and zinc in an activity-dependent manner.
* Zinc transporter deficits (mocha mutant mice) abolish vesicular zinc release.
* Experimental manipulations (EDTA/Ca-EDTA) revealed precise vesicular zinc dynamics.

---

#### 4. Electrophysiological Techniques

* Tetrode recordings capture local field potentials (LFPs) and single-unit spikes.
* Optogenetic thalamocortical stimulation shows state-dependent cortical responses.

**Experimental Insight:**

* NREM: largest cortical responses.
* Waking: muted responses.
* REM: intermediate responses.

---

#### 5. Spike Activity and Entropy

* Spikes are less organized in NREM compared to waking.
* High-activity neurons decrease firing, low-activity neurons increase (partial equalization).
* Entropy increases, indicating reduced predictability of spike patterns.

---

#### 6. Historical and Conceptual Insights

* Sleep has long been linked to insight (e.g., Kekulé, Mendeleev, Loewi).
* Sleep-specific activity patterns are crucial for cognitive processes not observed during wake.

**Key Insight:** The cortical network is highly dynamic, and sleep enables unique neuronal and cognitive functions essential for learning and memory.

---

### Summary Insight

* NREM sleep generates synchronized slow-wave cortical activity with distinct ON/OFF dynamics.
* Sleep-dependent memory consolidation involves both replay and synaptic homeostasis.
* Electrophysiology and optogenetics reveal dynamic state-dependent changes in cortical responses.
* Sleep is an active, highly regulated process with fundamental roles in cognition, synaptic plasticity, and memory.
