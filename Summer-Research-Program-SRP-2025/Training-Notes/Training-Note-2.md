# PI 5 — Prof. Takashi Matsuzaka

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Lipid Medicine          
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba        
**Research Model:** Mammalian systems (mouse models, cell lines)           
**Research Theme:** Development of strategies for metabolic disease management and treatment via qualitative control of fatty acids        

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory focuses on **lipid biology, energy metabolism, and endocrinology**, particularly how the quality of fatty acids (chain length and saturation/desaturation pattern) impacts health and disease. Key areas include:

* **Fatty acid metabolism**: Functional analysis of mammalian fatty acid elongase **Elovl6**, which elongates C12–C16 fatty acids to C18 products like stearic and vaccenic acid
* **Metabolic diseases**: Obesity, NAFLD/MASLD, diabetes, insulin resistance, atherosclerosis, heart failure
* **Cancer biology**: Breast cancer, bladder cancer, leukemia, glioblastoma
* **Mitochondrial function**: Cardiolipin remodeling by **ALCAT1** and its pathological implications
* **Neurodegenerative disease and sphingolipidosis**: Gaucher disease and Parkinson’s disease linked to GBA mutations
* **Therapeutic development**: Novel interventions via Elovl6 inhibition (antisense oligonucleotides, substrate reduction therapy)

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Fatty Acid Quality Control

* Fatty acids are essential for:

  1. Energy storage and metabolism
  2. Structural components of cell membranes
  3. Signaling molecules
  4. Protein recruitment and post-translational modifications

* Quality of fatty acids (chain length & saturation pattern) critically affects **metabolic and disease outcomes**

---

#### 2. Elovl6 Enzyme Function

* **Type:** Microsomal fatty acid elongase
* **Substrates:** C12, C14, and C16 fatty acids
* **Products:** C18 fatty acids, including stearic acid and vaccenic acid

**Key Insight:** Elovl6 acts as a **metabolic checkpoint**, regulating fatty acid chain elongation and composition

---

#### 3. Role in Metabolic Diseases

* **Obesity and MASLD/MASH**: Fatty acid quality affects lipotoxicity and tissue dysfunction

* **Elovl6 knockout mice**:

  * Protected against insulin resistance under high-fat, high-sucrose diets
  * Improved Akt phosphorylation
  * No reduction in obesity or hepatosteatosis, highlighting quality over quantity

* **Hepatocyte-specific Elovl6 deficiency**:

  * Improves insulin sensitivity
  * Alters cardiolipin composition in mitochondria
  * Restores mitochondrial function by reducing pathological remodeling mediated by ALCAT1

---

#### 4. Lipidomics and Mitochondrial Function

* **Cardiolipin (CL)**: Unique phospholipid in inner mitochondrial membrane
* **ALCAT1** catalyzes pathological CL remodeling → mitochondrial dysfunction
* **Elovl6 deficiency**:

  * Inhibits pathological remodeling
  * Restores mitochondrial oxygen consumption rate (OCR)
  * Increases very long-chain, highly unsaturated fatty acids in CL

**Key Insight:** Fatty acid composition directly affects mitochondrial structure and function

---

#### 5. Cancer and Elovl6

* Elovl6 activity supports proliferation in:

  * Breast cancer
  * Bladder cancer
  * Leukemia
  * Glioblastoma

* Mechanism: modulation of lipid composition affecting energy supply, membrane integrity, and signaling

---

#### 6. Neurodegeneration and Sphingolipidosis

* **Gaucher disease**: Lysosomal storage disorder caused by sphingolipid accumulation
* **Elovl6 inhibition** reduces hexosylceramide accumulation and improves lipid profiles in mouse models
* Links between **GBA mutations**, Gaucher disease, and Parkinson’s disease suggest therapeutic potential of Elovl6 inhibitors

---

### Summary Insight

* **Elovl6** is a master regulator of fatty acid chain elongation and composition, acting as a metabolic checkpoint
* **Hepatic Elovl6 deficiency** improves insulin sensitivity and mitochondrial function independent of fat accumulation
* Control of **lipid quality** offers therapeutic avenues for metabolic syndrome, cancer, and neurodegenerative diseases
* Research integrates **lipidomics, single-cell RNA-seq, translational studies**, and biomarker discovery to understand and treat diverse pathologies

# PI 6 — Prof. Tomonori Isobe

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Applied Medical Physics   
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba      
**Research Model:** Human and animal models; medical imaging and radiation systems    
**Research Theme:** Medical Physics at the Frontiers of Healthcare: Advancing Radiotherapy, Imaging, and Safety   

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory aims to develop **advanced medical technologies** through the integration of **physics and medicine**. Main research areas include:

* **Radiation therapy**: improving treatment precision and sparing healthy tissues
* **Medical imaging and analysis**: CT, PET, and PET-CT fusion for accurate anatomical and functional assessment
* **Artificial intelligence applications**: for enhanced diagnosis, image reconstruction, and therapy planning
* **Biometric measurement technologies**: real-time monitoring of physiological parameters
* **Radiation protection and safety management**: optimization of exposure limits and safety protocols

The lab integrates **fundamental physics, computational modeling, and clinical translation**, collaborating internationally to enhance patient care and safety.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Radiation and Radioactivity

* **Radiation:** high-energy particles or electromagnetic waves
* **Radioactivity:** property of a substance that emits radiation

**Types of radiation:**

| Type               | Charge | Mass | Examples               |
| ------------------ | ------ | ---- | ---------------------- |
| Electromagnetic    | none   | none | X-ray, γ-ray           |
| Charged particle   | yes    | yes  | α, β, electron, proton |
| Uncharged particle | no     | yes  | neutron                |

**Half-life:** the time for half of a radioactive substance to decay (I-131 ~8 days, Cs-137 ~30 years)

**Units:**

* **Becquerel (Bq):** radioactivity
* **Gray (Gy):** absorbed dose
* **Sievert (Sv):** biological effect risk

**Radiation penetration:**

* Alpha → stopped by paper
* Beta → stopped by ~1 cm plastic
* X-ray/γ → requires thick lead

**LD50/60:** ~4 Gy whole-body dose

---

#### 2. Radiation Interaction and Cellular Effects

* Radiation ionizes molecules → DNA and cellular damage
* Thermal effect negligible: 10 Gy → ~0.0024 °C rise in 1 kg water

**Charged particle beams:** alpha particles, protons, electrons
**Lowest X-ray absorption tissue:** lung

---

#### 3. Imaging Techniques

**X-ray imaging:**

* Uses external radiation source
* Produces morphological images reflecting tissue density differences

**Nuclear medicine imaging:**

* Detects gamma rays emitted from radioactive tracers inside the body
* **PET-CT fusion:** combines functional PET with anatomical CT → precise localization of lesions and differentiation from physiological uptake

**CT gantry advancement:** slip-ring allows continuous rotation, reducing scan time

---

#### 4. Radiation Therapy Techniques

* **External beam shaping:** Multi-leaf collimator conforms dose to tumor geometry

* **Proton therapy vs X-ray therapy:**

  * Protons → peak dose at tumor depth, sparing beyond-tumor tissue
  * X-rays → dose along entry and exit path

* **Brachytherapy:** internal source irradiation

* **Clinical benefit:** preserves organ function and patient quality of life

**Example — Laryngeal cancer:**

| Treatment | Outcome                                                         |
| --------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Surgery   | Removal of larynx → cannot speak, smell; permanent tracheostomy |
| Radiation | Targeted irradiation → organ function preserved; maintains QOL  |

---

#### 5. Radiation Safety Principles

* **Time:** minimize exposure duration
* **Distance:** maximize distance from source
* **Shielding:** use protective barriers
* **Dosimetry:** monitor received dose with personal devices

**Applications beyond medicine:**

* Airport baggage screening
* Non-destructive industrial testing
* Agricultural use (e.g., potato sprout prevention)
* Radiometric dating

---

### Summary Insight

* Medical physics combines radiation, imaging, and safety to **enhance cancer therapy precision and diagnostic accuracy**
* PET-CT fusion and proton therapy illustrate **functional-anatomical integration and targeted treatment**
* Radiation safety is critical; ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) is applied
* Research spans **basic physics, computational modeling, and clinical application**, aiming to improve patient outcomes and reduce risks

# PI 7 — Prof. Masashi Yanagisawa

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Yanagisawa / Funato Laboratory   
**Institute:** International Institute for Integrative Sleep Medicine (WPI-IIIS), University of Tsukuba    
**Research Model:** Mouse models, human sleep studies, in-home EEG systems    
**Research Theme:** Molecular Mechanisms Regulating Sleep/Wakefulness Behavior   

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates the **molecular and neural mechanisms that regulate sleep and wakefulness**.

Key research directions include:

* **Discovery of sleep-regulating molecules**, including the neuropeptide **orexin**, a central regulator of sleep/wake stability
* **Neural circuit mechanisms** controlling REM and NREM sleep transitions
* **Forward genetics in mice**: starting from phenotype → identifying causal genes
* **Large-scale genetic screening** to uncover novel regulators of sleep quantity and quality
* **Real-time visualization and manipulation of neuronal activity** in freely behaving mice
* **Translational sleep research** linking basic neuroscience to metabolic and lifestyle diseases

The lab bridges **basic neuroscience, genetics, pharmacology, and clinical sleep medicine** to decipher the fundamental mystery of sleep.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: The Mystery of Sleep

We spend nearly **one-third of our lives asleep**, yet:

* What does sleep do?
* How is sleep/wake regulated?

Sleep disorders are:

* A major societal problem
* A risk factor for metabolic syndrome and lifestyle diseases

Sleep appears evolutionarily conserved:

* “Sleep is invented before the brains”
* A clear **phylogenetic signal** exists in daily sleep time among terrestrial mammals

---

#### 2. Forward Genetics Approach

**Forward genetics strategy:**

1. Start with a **phenotypic trait** (e.g., abnormal sleep duration)
2. Identify the **gene responsible**

Key sleep indicator:

* **NREM sleep delta power** → indicator of **sleep pressure**

This approach enables unbiased discovery of genes directly regulating sleep/wake behavior.

---

#### 3. Orexin: A Breakthrough Discovery

The lab discovered the neuropeptide **orexin**, a key regulator of wakefulness.

**Clinical significance:**

* Loss of orexin neurons → narcolepsy
* Orexin stabilizes wakefulness and prevents sudden REM transitions

---

#### 4. REM Sleep Gating and Dopamine Circuits

Recent findings identified:

* A new role for **dopamine in the amygdala**
* REM sleep gating mechanisms in mice

Key neural circuit components:

* Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA-DA neurons)
* Nucleus Accumbens (NAc)
* Basolateral Amygdala (BLA)

These circuits regulate emotional and motivational influences on sleep.

---

#### 5. Molecular Regulation of Sleep Amount

Two major regulatory dimensions:

1. **Sleep need (hours)** → builds gradually
2. **Sleep/wake switching (seconds)** → rapid state transitions

Key molecular regulator:

* **SIK3** → regulates sleep amount and quality across brain regions

Supporting theory:

* **Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis (SHY)**

  * Increased synaptic strength during wake
  * Sleep (especially NREM) restores synaptic balance

Experimental insight:

* Enlargement of pyramidal neuron spines in prefrontal cortex →

  * Increased NREM sleep
  * Increased EEG delta power

---

#### 6. Translational and Clinical Applications

**Orexin receptors as drug targets:**

Approved orexin antagonists for insomnia:

* Suvorexant (Merck)
* Lemborexant (Eisai)
* Daridorexant (Idorsia)

Orexin agonists (for narcolepsy and hypersomnia):

* Ongoing development
* Phase 3 trials: Oveporexton (Takeda)

---

#### 7. Real-World Sleep Studies

Large-scale observational studies using **in-home EEG (InSomnograf)** revealed:

* Objective sleep quality strongly correlates with health indices
* Subjective perception does not always match objective sleep

Example:

A woman complaining of severe insomnia showed:

* Objectively healthy hypnogram
* Only brief WASO (Wake After Sleep Onset)

This demonstrates **sleep-state misperception**, highlighting the importance of objective measurement.

---

### Summary Insight

* Sleep regulation operates at **molecular, circuit, genetic, and behavioral levels**
* Orexin is a master regulator of wake stability
* NREM delta power reflects sleep pressure
* Forward genetics enables unbiased discovery of sleep genes
* Translational research has already led to approved insomnia drugs
* Sleep research integrates neuroscience, metabolism, psychiatry, and public health

# PI 8 — Prof. Mamiko Sakata

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Division of Advanced Hemato-Oncology   
**Institute:** Department of Hematology & Division of Advanced Hemato-Oncology, University of Tsukuba    
**Research Model:** Genetically engineered and disease mouse models; primary human blood and tumor specimens; single-cell RNA sequencing–based immune profiling.    
**Research Theme:** Oncoimmunology in Blood and Solid Cancers   

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **cancer pathogenesis and tumor microenvironment regulation** by integrating clinical genomics, single-cell transcriptomics, and disease mouse models, focusing on:

* **Age-related clonal hematopoiesis (CH)** and its role in cancer initiation
* **Multi-step tumorigenesis** in hematologic malignancies
* **Tumor–immune cell interactions** in both blood and solid cancers
* **Inflammatory microenvironment remodeling** driven by mutated hematopoietic clones
* Translational integration of **data science + mechanistic validation** (“Bench to Bedside”)

Key discoveries include identification of **Gly17Val RHOA** and **TET2 mutations** in Angioimmunoblastic T-cell lymphoma (AITL), establishing a genomic foundation for multi-step tumorigenesis models.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Data-Driven Cancer Biology

A major transformation in cancer research occurred with the emergence of **Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)**:

* Whole-genome sequencing
* Whole-transcriptome profiling
* Rapid identification of genetic abnormalities
* Transition from hypothesis-driven to **data-driven discovery**

NGS now enables:

1. Comprehensive mutation detection
2. Transcriptomic profiling
3. Analysis at **single-cell resolution**
4. Integration of clinical genomic datasets with computational biology

**Key Insight:** Modern cancer biology relies on large-scale genomic data to uncover mechanisms previously inaccessible through classical approaches.

---

#### 2. Clonal Hematopoiesis (CH)

**Definition:**
Age-related expansion of hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) clones harboring somatic mutations.

**Common mutated genes:**

* DNMT3A
* TET2
* ASXL1

**Epidemiology:**

* ~10% prevalence in individuals around age 60
* Frequency increases with aging

**Biological Process:**

1. Somatic mutation occurs in HSC
2. Mutated clone expands
3. Blood system gradually replaced by mutated lineage
4. Alters immune and inflammatory landscape

---

#### 3. Clinical Implications of Clonal Hematopoiesis

CH serves as a risk factor for:

* Hematologic malignancies
* Lifestyle-related diseases (e.g., ischemic heart disease)
* Potential modulation of solid tumor progression

**Technology enabling discovery:**
Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS), including single-cell applications.

**Key Insight:** Aging-associated somatic evolution in HSCs reshapes systemic immunity and predisposes to disease.

---

#### 4. Multi-Step Tumorigenesis in AITL

Using NGS technologies, the lab identified:

* **G17V RHOA mutation** (tumor-specific)
* **TET2 mutation** (clonal hematopoiesis-associated)

Published in Nature Genetics and Nature Cell Biology.

**Proposed Model:**

**Step 1 – Clonal Hematopoiesis Stage**

* Mutations in DNMT3A / TET2 arise in HSCs
* Mutated immune progenitors expand

**Step 2 – Tumor-Specific Transformation**

* Acquisition of RHOA G17V mutation
* Transformation into malignant T follicular helper cells

**Key Concept:**
AITL develops through sequential accumulation of mutations across distinct cellular compartments.

---

#### 5. Clonal Hematopoiesis and Tumor Microenvironment

**In Blood Cancers:**

* TET2-mutated immune cells enhance lymphoma-supportive microenvironment
* Promote inflammatory signaling
* Stimulate malignant T follicular helper cells

**In Solid Cancers:**

Example: Lung cancer models

* Tet2-mutated granulocyte/myeloid-like cells
* Secrete S100A8/9
* Promote tumor growth and angiogenesis

**Key Insight:**
CH-derived immune cells are not passive bystanders; they actively reshape tumor ecosystems.

---

#### 6. Current Research Objective: Oncoimmunology

Central question:

How do clonal hematopoiesis-derived immune cells influence tumor progression in both hematologic and solid cancers?

Focus areas:

* Tumor microenvironment heterogeneity
* Immune cell lineage tracing
* Inflammatory signaling networks
* Functional validation in disease mouse models

---

#### 7. Experimental & Computational Strategy

**Sample Sources:**

* Clinical cancer specimens
* Disease mouse models

**Single-Cell RNA Sequencing Workflow:**

* 10x Genomics Chromium 3′ / 5′ platform
* Illumina sequencing systems
* Supercomputing-based analysis

**Single-Cell RNA Bioinformatic Pipeline Used:**

* **CellRanger** → Alignment & quantification
* **Seurat** → Quality control, clustering, differential expression
* **CellPhoneDB** → Cell–cell interaction inference
* **GSVA** → Pathway enrichment analysis
* **Monocle3** → Trajectory analysis

---

#### 8. Representative Achievement

In follicular lymphoma, the lab:

* Established a **single-cell atlas**
* Defined microenvironmental architecture
* Clarified immune–tumor interactions
* Published in Nature Cell Biology (2022)

---

### Summary Insight

* Cancer evolution involves both tumor-intrinsic mutations and systemic hematopoietic alterations
* Age-related clonal hematopoiesis alters immune cell composition
* Mutated immune cells can enhance tumor progression
* Single-cell genomics is essential to resolve tumor microenvironment complexity
* Integration of big data analysis and in vivo validation enables translational cancer research

The laboratory integrates:

**Aging → Clonal Hematopoiesis → Immune Remodeling → Tumor Microenvironment → Cancer Progression**

This framework positions the lab at the interface of:

* Hematology
* Cancer genomics
* Single-cell transcriptomics
* Oncoimmunology
* Translational medicine
