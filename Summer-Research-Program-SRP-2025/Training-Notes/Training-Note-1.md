# PI 1 — Prof. Kenji Irie

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Laboratory of Molecular Cell Biology   
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba   
**Research Model:** *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* (Budding yeast)    
**Research Theme:** Post-transcriptional Regulation of Gene Expression in Yeast    

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **post-transcriptional regulation of gene expression** in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, focusing on:

* **RNA-binding proteins** and their roles in mRNA processing, localization, translation, and stability
* **Cellular organization**: mRNA localization and local translation controlling cell polarity, asymmetric division, and cell fate
* **Stress response regulation**: signaling pathways modulating ER stress
* **Membrane dynamics**: vesicle docking during prospore membrane formation

**Key molecular regulators** such as the **Ccr4-Not complex** are studied as central hubs integrating these processes.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Post-Transcriptional Regulation

* Regulation beyond transcription, from RNA processing to protein activity
* Major steps of gene expression control:

  1. Transcriptional control
  2. RNA processing (e.g., alternative splicing)
  3. RNA transport and localization (nucleus → cytoplasm, or cytoplasmic localization)
  4. Translational control
  5. mRNA degradation
  6. Protein activity regulation
* Post-transcriptional regulation enables **rapid, dynamic protein synthesis control** without altering transcription rates

---

#### 2. mRNA Structure and Functional Regulation

**5′ Cap:**

* 7-methylguanosine modification
* Essential for translation initiation
* Protects mRNA from exonuclease degradation

**3′ Poly(A) Tail:**

* Enhances stability and translation efficiency
* Binds Poly(A)-Binding Protein (PABP)

**Key Insight:** Poly(A) tail length directly affects mRNA lifespan and translation efficiency

---

#### 3. Translation Initiation Mechanisms

**Cap-Dependent Translation:**

* Requires 5′ cap and poly(A) tail
* Involves initiation factors: eIF4E (cap-binding), eIF4G (scaffold), PABP

**IRES-Dependent Translation:**

* Cap-independent, commonly used in viral RNAs
* Uses Internal Ribosome Entry Sites (IRES)

---

#### 4. mRNA Degradation and Deadenylation

* Initiated by poly(A) tail shortening
* **Major deadenylase complexes:**

  * **Ccr4-Not:** primary regulator, integrates environmental signaling, controls gene-specific decay
  * **Pan2-Pan3:** initial trimming of poly(A) tails
* Followed by either:

  * 3′ → 5′ exonucleolytic degradation
  * Decapping + 5′ → 3′ degradation

**Experimental Insight:**

* ccr4Δ mutants grow poorly; ccr4Δ pan2Δ double mutants show even stronger growth defects → highlights essential regulatory role

---

#### 5. Ccr4-Not Complex: Functional Role

* Central hub for mRNA decay and translational control
* Negatively regulates specific mRNAs (e.g., LRG1, CLB6)
* Coordinates **competition between translation and decay**, integrating signals from RNA-binding proteins and miRNAs

---

#### 6. microRNA-Mediated Regulation

* miRNAs: short, single-stranded RNAs (~21–25 nt)
* Bind 3′ UTRs of target mRNAs
* Recruit Ccr4-Not complex via RISC for translational repression or degradation
* Implicated in:

  * Cancer biology
  * Epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT)
  * Cell differentiation

**Key Insight:** miRNA-mediated regulation exemplifies **post-transcriptional fine-tuning** of gene expression

---

### Summary Insight

* Post-transcriptional regulation integrates mRNA structure, decay, translation, and miRNA-mediated control
* The **Ccr4-Not complex** is a master regulator coordinating environmental and developmental cues
* Understanding these mechanisms in yeast provides **generalizable insights** into eukaryotic gene regulation and disease contexts

# PI 2 — Prof. Atsushi Kawaguchi

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Molecular Virology    
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba     
**Research Theme:** Molecular Mechanisms of Viral Replication and Pathogenesis     
**Research Model:** Viral infections in human and animal models, including **avian influenza virus** and **SARS-CoV-2**     

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **virus-host interactions** that determine pathogenicity, species specificity, and immune evasion, with emphasis on:

* **Molecular mechanisms of viral replication** in human and animal cells
* **Emerging infectious diseases**: spillover from wild/domestic animals to humans
* **Innate immune responses**: host factors controlling viral infection and pathogenicity
* **Host-pathogen interactions**: viral adaptation to host factors, immune escape, and excessive inflammatory responses
* **Animal models**: genetically modified mice for studying viral pathogenicity

Key focus is on understanding **how viruses replicate, spread, and cause disease**, and how **host factors like MxA protein** mediate viral restriction and immune response.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Viral Emergence and Zoonosis

* Emerging viruses are transmitted from wild animals or livestock (**spillover/host jump**)
* Examples: Avian influenza, SARS-CoV-2, Ebola, Rabies, BSE, Lyme disease
* Respiratory viral infections can lead to secondary bacterial pneumonia (commensal nasal bacteria often implicated)
* Viruses are **acellular** and depend entirely on host cells for replication (e.g., poliovirus is “dead” outside host cells)

---

#### 2. Viral Structure and Host Adaptation

* Viruses consist of genome + protein capsid ± lipid envelope
* Typically ~100 nm, ~1000 times smaller than mammalian cells
* Influenza A virus encodes ~12 proteins; SARS-CoV-2 encodes structural and non-structural proteins for replication
* Structural components like PB2 subunit recognize host mRNA caps for viral transcription (**cap-snatching mechanism**)
* Host adaptation driven by mutations in viral polymerase subunits (PB2, PA, HA, NP) → determines mammalian adaptation and pathogenicity

---

#### 3. Viral Replication and Pathogenesis

* Viruses hijack host machineries for replication, transcription, and assembly

* Viral polymerase activity correlates with host adaptation (avian vs human influenza strains)

* Influenza vs SARS-CoV-2:

  * Influenza A replication in nucleus; SARS-CoV-2 forms replication complexes in cytoplasmic invadopodia
  * SARS-CoV-2 NSP8 localizes to invadopodia, facilitating spread
  * Viremia: viral particles enter bloodstream, infect endothelial cells

* Host defense mechanisms: **MxA protein** in epithelial cells restricts viral replication

* Delayed immune activation (e.g., IL-1β induction) can result from viral escape mutations

---

#### 4. Experimental Approaches

* **Structural analysis:** Cryo-EM of viral polymerase (Titan Krios)
* **Functional assays:** Assessment of viral polymerase activity and host adaptation
* **Animal models:** Genetically modified mice (hMx-Tg) for studying innate immune responses
* Integration of structural, molecular, and in vivo data to link **viral replication to pathogenicity**

---

### Summary Insight

* Viral pathogenesis depends on both **intrinsic viral mechanisms** (polymerase activity, cap-snatching, host factor hijacking) and **host responses** (MxA-mediated restriction, immune signaling)
* Emerging viruses often arise via **host jumps** and adaptive mutations in intermediate hosts
* Understanding molecular details of **virus-host interactions** can inform therapeutic and preventive strategies
* Comparative studies of influenza A and SARS-CoV-2 reveal **shared and distinct mechanisms of replication, immune evasion, and dissemination**

# PI 3 — Prof. Seiya Mizuno

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Laboratory Animal Science    
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba   
**Research Theme:** Experimental Genetics Using Genome-Edited Mouse Models     
**Research Model:** Genetically modified mice (GMM), tissue-specific in vivo genome editing     

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory develops and utilizes **genetically modified mouse models** to study human diseases and fundamental biological mechanisms. Key areas of focus include:

* **Tissue-specific genome editing**: Developing in vivo CRISPR-based techniques
* **Cardiac disease research**: Elucidating the molecular basis of diseases caused by point mutations or splicing abnormalities
* **Germ cell biology**: Investigating mechanisms of sperm and oocyte maintenance, maturation, and early embryogenesis
* **Creation and analysis of genetically modified animals (GMMs)** to support biomedical and life sciences research

**Key insight:** Laboratory mice provide **rapid, prolific, and highly controlled experimental models**, critical for functional and mechanistic studies in vivo.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Role of Animal Models in Medical Research

* Animal models are essential for understanding **human disease mechanisms** and for in vivo evaluation of gene function

* Nobel Prize statistics (1901–2021):

  * 89% of awarded research topics used animal models
  * 84% of winners conducted research using laboratory animals

* Mice are **preferred due to prolific reproduction and short life cycles**

  * 5–15 offspring per litter
  * Pregnancy: 19.5 days; sexual maturity: 8 weeks
  * ~4 generations/year

**Key Insight:** Genetically modified mice (GMMs) provide direct evidence of gene function in vivo

---

#### 2. Functional vs. Mechanistic Analysis

* **Functional analysis:** Determines what a gene or protein does
* **Mechanistic analysis:** Explains how it works at molecular/cellular levels
* Animal models allow both types of analysis in controlled physiological contexts

---

#### 3. Forward and Reverse Genetics

* **Forward genetics:** Start from a phenotype → identify responsible gene
* **Reverse genetics:** Start from a gene → analyze resultant phenotype
* **Knockout (KO) mice:** Gene turned off to study its function

**Key Insight:** GMMs enable precise control of gene function for both forward and reverse genetics approaches

---

#### 4. Techniques in Creating Genetically Modified Mice

* **Gene targeting:** Removal or modification of endogenous genes

* **Chimeric mice:** Intra-species embryo-embryo chimeras

* **CRISPR-Cas9 injections** into fertilized embryos to produce:

  * Homozygous KO mice
  * Fluorescent reporter mice for visualizing cell localization and dynamics

* Laboratory has created numerous GMMs over 6 years, contributing to **advanced medical research**

---

#### 5. Applications of Genetically Modified Mice

* **Disease modeling:** Cardiovascular, metabolic, neurological, and developmental disorders
* **Molecular mechanism studies:** Gene function, splicing abnormalities, tissue-specific gene regulation
* **Reproductive biology:** Germ cell development, oocyte and sperm maturation, pre-implantation embryo studies
* **Support for biomedical research:** Validating therapeutic targets, drug testing, and understanding human disease mechanisms

**Key Insight:** GMMs allow **precise in vivo analysis** linking genotype to phenotype, providing insights impossible to obtain from in vitro studies alone

---

### Summary Insight

* Prof. Mizuno’s laboratory leverages **genome-edited mice** to uncover functional and mechanistic insights in mammalian biology
* Development of **tissue-specific genome editing techniques** enhances the resolution of in vivo studies
* GMMs provide a **powerful platform** for understanding human diseases, testing therapies, and supporting translational research

# PI 4 — Prof. Kazuya Morikawa

### Laboratory Profile

**Laboratory:** Infection Biology (Bacteriology)     
**Institute:** University of Tsukuba    
**Research Theme:** Nucleoid Dynamics in Bacterial Stress Response     
**Research Model:** *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Escherichia coli*, *Mycobacterium smegmatis*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Bacillus subtilis*      

---

### Research Focus of the Laboratory

The laboratory investigates **bacterial survival strategies** in the context of infectious diseases and symbiosis, focusing on:

* **Population heterogeneity:** Minor subpopulations expressing *esp* genes involved in horizontal gene transfer and antibiotic resistance
* **Nucleoid dynamics:** Structural changes and “clogging” of the nucleoid under stress conditions (e.g., oxidative stress)
* **Membrane interactions:** Relationship between nucleoid structure and membrane-associated proteins
* **Anti-virulence strategies:** Identification of potential targets for anti-virulence drugs

**Key insight:** Understanding nucleoid dynamics and subpopulation gene expression reveals **stress tolerance mechanisms and infection processes** in bacteria.

---

### Lecture Notes

#### 1. Core Concept: Nucleoid Dynamics

* The **bacterial nucleoid** is a dynamic DNA-protein complex critical for gene regulation and stress response
* **Nucleoid clogging:** DNA-binding proteins (e.g., Dps, MrgA) condense DNA to protect against oxidative stress
* **Ferroxidase activity:** Converts Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → Fe³⁺ + OH⁻ + OH·, preventing hydroxyl radical formation via Fenton reaction
* Nucleoid clogging and ferroxidase activity can be **mutationally dissected** to study DNA protection mechanisms

---

#### 2. Bacterial Stress Response and Gene Expression

* Minor subpopulations express **esp (expression in minor subpopulation) genes**, which may:

  * Enable **horizontal gene transfer**
  * Confer **antibiotic resistance**

* Nucleoid clogging affects gene expression near the **origin of replication (ori)**, contributing to **stress tolerance and virulence**

* DNA-binding proteins (NAPs) show **diverse sequences across species**, but functionally similar gene sets are maintained

**Key Insight:** Nucleoid structure modulates transcriptional regulation under environmental stresses

---

#### 3. Comparative Nucleoid Studies

* **E. coli vs. S. aureus:**

  * E. coli: nucleoid compaction during stationary phase for DNA protection
  * S. aureus: nucleoid clogging under oxidative stress, mediated by MrgA and DpsD18
  * Different nucleoid architectures reflect **species-specific environmental adaptations**

* Observed using **atomic force microscopy (AFM)**:

  * Lag phase → fibrous nucleoid
  * Stationary/oxidative stress → compacted or clogged nucleoid (~80 nm fiber)

---

#### 4. Experimental Approaches

* **Fractionation:** Membrane and nucleoid isolation followed by protein identification
* **csNAPs (contaminant-subtracted nucleoid-associated proteins):** Identified >700 proteins across multiple bacterial species
* **Mutagenesis:** Dissecting ferroxidase activity vs. DNA-binding in Dps/MrgA
* **Functional assays:** Stress tolerance and gene expression profiling under oxidative conditions

**Key Insight:** Proteins enriched in nucleoid fraction, including translation machinery, suggest nucleoid dynamics can influence global gene expression

---

#### 5. Implications for Infection Biology

* Nucleoid clogging contributes to **bacterial survival during host infection**
* Minor subpopulation dynamics affect **antibiotic resistance and virulence gene transfer**
* Understanding nucleoid and membrane interactions can guide **anti-virulence drug development**

**Key Insight:** Nucleoid dynamics represent a **critical bacterial adaptation mechanism** relevant to pathogenicity and stress resilience

---

### Summary Insight

* Prof. Morikawa’s laboratory elucidates **how bacterial nucleoids respond to environmental stresses** and modulate gene expression
* Insights from minor subpopulations (*esp* genes) reveal **population heterogeneity in survival and antibiotic resistance**
* Integration of nucleoid structure, protein function, and stress response provides a **mechanistic framework** for infection biology and anti-virulence strategies
