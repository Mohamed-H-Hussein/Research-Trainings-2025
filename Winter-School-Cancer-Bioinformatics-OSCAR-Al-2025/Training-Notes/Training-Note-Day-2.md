## Session: Tech Fusion Meets Big Data — A Novel Role for NPM1 in Cancer Pathology

**Speaker:** Prof. Dr. Hiba El Hajj — American University of Beirut (AUB), Lebanon  
**Type:** Lecture 

#### 1. Background

Cancer metastasis remains the **leading cause of cancer-related mortality**. Understanding the molecular drivers of metastasis is essential for developing targeted therapies.

The session explored the **role of Nucleophosmin 1 (NPM1)** in tumor progression and metastatic behavior.

---

#### 2. Cancer Metastasis Overview

Metastasis involves multiple biological steps:

* Genetic and epigenetic alterations
* **Epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT)**
* Intravasation into the bloodstream
* Immune system evasion
* Extravasation into distant organs
* Adaptation to new microenvironments

---

#### 3. NPM1 in Cancer Biology

NPM1 is involved in several cellular processes including:

* **Ribosome biogenesis**
* Regulation of gene expression
* Cell proliferation and survival

Elevated NPM1 expression was observed in aggressive cancers, including:

* **Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC)**
* **Colorectal cancer (CRC)**

High NPM1 levels correlate with increased:

* tumor proliferation
* cell migration
* invasive potential

---

#### 4. Experimental Findings

Research results demonstrated that:

* **NPM1 knockdown reduces metastatic capacity** of cancer cells.
* Silencing NPM1 suppresses metastasis to organs such as **lungs, liver, brain, and bone**.
* Downregulation of NPM1 in CRC cells decreases tumor growth and prolongs survival in mouse models.

RNA-seq analysis of patient samples revealed **higher NPM1 expression in metastatic tissues**.

---

#### 5. Key Takeaways

* NPM1 may function as a **key regulator of cancer metastasis**.
* Targeting NPM1-related pathways could provide **novel therapeutic strategies**.

## Session: Single-Cell RNA-seq and Spatial Transcriptomics Integration for Tumor Microenvironment Analysis 

**Speaker:** Prof. Dr. Kais Ghedira — Institut Pasteur de Tunis (IPT), Tunisia  
**Type:** Lecture 

#### 1. Importance of Transcriptomics

Transcriptomics provides insights into the **functional state of cells**, reflecting active gene expression patterns.

It bridges the gap between:

* **genomic potential**
* **protein function**

---

#### 2. Limitations of Bulk RNA-seq

Traditional bulk RNA sequencing measures **average gene expression across mixed cell populations**.

This results in the **averaging problem**, where signals from rare but important cells are masked.

---

#### 3. Emergence of Single-Cell RNA Sequencing

Single-cell RNA-seq enables analysis of **gene expression at the resolution of individual cells**.

Common platforms include:

* 10x Genomics
* Drop-seq
* Smart-seq2

Typical scRNA-seq analyses include:

* quality control
* dimensionality reduction
* clustering
* cell type annotation
* differential expression analysis

---

#### 4. Spatial Transcriptomics

Spatial transcriptomics preserves **tissue architecture**, allowing researchers to determine:

* where genes are expressed
* which cell types interact
* how tumor niches are organized

---

#### 5. Integrating scRNA-seq and Spatial Data

Combining both technologies allows researchers to obtain:

* **high-resolution cell identity (scRNA-seq)**
* **tissue-level spatial context (ST)**

This integration enables deeper understanding of the **tumor microenvironment (TME)** and cellular interactions.

---

#### Key Takeaways

* Integrated multi-modal analysis improves **tumor microenvironment characterization**.
* Spatial transcriptomics is becoming a critical tool for **precision oncology research**.

## Session: Drug Design in Cancer Research

**Speaker:** Prof. Dr. Ounssema Khamessi — Institut Pasteur de Tunis (IPT), Tunisia  
**Type:** Lecture 

#### 1. Overview

Cancer drug design focuses on developing **therapies that selectively target cancer cells** while minimizing toxicity to normal tissues.

This field integrates **molecular biology, structural biology, and bioinformatics**.

---

#### 2. Key Targets in Cancer Therapy

Drug targets include:

* receptors
* enzymes
* cell-cycle regulators
* signaling pathway proteins

---

#### 3. Drug Design Approaches

Two major strategies are commonly used:

##### Structure-Based Drug Design (SBDD)

* Requires knowledge of the **3D structure of the biological target**.
* Uses techniques such as:

  * molecular docking
  * virtual screening
  * molecular dynamics simulations

##### Ligand-Based Drug Design (LBDD)

* Used when the **target structure is unknown**.
* Relies on known active compounds to design improved analogues.
* Methods include:

  * QSAR analysis
  * pharmacophore modeling
  * molecular similarity analysis

---

#### Key Takeaways

* Computational tools accelerate **drug discovery and optimization**.
* Rational drug design supports the development of **precision oncology therapies**.

## Session: AI-Driven Discovery — Computational Revolution in Drug Development  

**Speaker:** Prof. Dr. Mohamed Hamed — German University in Cairo (GUC) & University Medical Center Rostock (UMR), Germany   
**Type:** Lecture 

### Overview

The session introduced how **Artificial Intelligence and computational biology** are transforming modern drug development. Computational approaches allow researchers to analyze biological data, predict therapeutic targets, and accelerate drug discovery pipelines.

The session focused on the **drug development pipeline** and the role of **bioinformatics, machine learning, and computational modeling** in each stage.

---

## Drug Development Pipeline

The drug discovery process includes several key stages, many of which are increasingly supported by **computational and AI-driven methods**.

#### 1. Target Identification

The first stage aims to identify biological molecules involved in disease mechanisms that can serve as potential therapeutic targets.

Approaches discussed included:

* **Bioinformatics analysis**
* **Reverse docking**
* **Computational chemical biology**

Additional AI-supported strategies included:

* **ML-based Polygenic Priority Score** for ranking potential targets
* **Target triaging pipelines** to prioritize promising candidates
* **Large-scale in-house studies** analyzing therapeutic outcomes
* **AI-based literature search engines** for continuously retrieving relevant publications

The literature mining system operates using:

* Therapeutic-area keywords (e.g., CIIM)
* Higher weighting for **high-impact journals** such as *Nature*, *Cell*, and *Science*
* Automated searches performed **twice per week**

These approaches help researchers identify promising targets more efficiently by integrating **genomic data, biological knowledge, and scientific literature**.

---

#### 2. Target Validation

Once potential targets are identified, they must be evaluated to confirm their biological and therapeutic relevance.

Computational approaches discussed included:

* **Druggability prediction**
* **Computational systems biology**
* Structural and pathway analysis

A **Comprehensive Target Report (CTR)** is generated to summarize available evidence supporting the therapeutic potential of the target.

Additional resources used during validation include:

**Tumor Model Characterization (TMC) Portal**

This platform provides:

* systematic characterization of commonly used **mouse tumor models**
* support for selecting appropriate models for **in-vivo proof-of-concept experiments**
* integration with **TCGA indications**

Another tool discussed was the **Single-Cell Immune Atlas (SC Immune Atlas)**, which helps analyze immune cell populations relevant to tumor biology.

---

#### 3. Lead Discovery

Once a target has been validated, the next step is identifying molecules that interact with the target.

Computational approaches include:

* **Virtual screening**
* **De-novo molecular design**

These techniques allow the exploration of large chemical spaces and the identification of promising candidate molecules for further testing.

---

#### 4. Lead Optimization

Promising molecules must then be optimized to improve their pharmacological properties.

Methods mentioned during the session included:

* **Scaffold hopping**
* **Structure-Activity Relationship (SAR) analysis**
* **In-situ molecular design**

These approaches help improve properties such as:

* binding affinity
* selectivity
* stability
* drug-like characteristics

---

#### 5. Preclinical Testing

Before human trials, drug candidates undergo extensive preclinical evaluation.

Computational approaches discussed included:

* **In-silico ADMET prediction**
* **DMPK simulation** (Drug Metabolism and Pharmacokinetics)
* **Computational systems biology modeling**

These methods help predict toxicity, metabolism, and pharmacokinetics before experimental testing.

---

#### 6. Clinical Trials

Following successful preclinical evaluation, candidate drugs proceed to **clinical trials**, where safety and therapeutic efficacy are tested in human participants.

---

## Additional Research Topics Discussed

The session also highlighted several research frameworks used in computational drug discovery.

#### Biomarker Discovery from Multi-Omics Data

Integrative analysis of multi-omics datasets can help identify **biomarkers associated with therapeutic response or disease progression**.

These biomarkers support:

* patient stratification
* therapy selection
* precision medicine approaches

---

#### Drug Combination Studies

Research projects may evaluate **drug combinations** to enhance therapeutic effectiveness.

An example discussed involved evaluating **mono-therapy versus combined inhibition strategies**, using computational and experimental models to measure:

* cell proliferation
* pathway activity
* treatment response over time

---

## Key Takeaways

* AI and computational biology play a major role in **modern drug discovery pipelines**.
* Bioinformatics tools assist in **target identification, validation, and biomarker discovery**.
* Integrating **multi-omics data and computational modeling** improves therapeutic development strategies.

## Session: Stereotypes — Advances of AI for Healthcare (Pro vs Contra)

**Speakers:**

* Mohammed Salem — German University in Cairo
* Hiba El Hajj — American University of Beirut

**Type:** Panel Discussion / Debate

---

### Overview

This session explored **contrasting perspectives on the growing role of Artificial Intelligence in healthcare**.

The discussion examined both the **potential benefits (Pro)** and the **ethical, technical, and social challenges (Contra)** associated with deploying AI in clinical environments.

---

### Pro Perspective — Opportunities of AI in Healthcare

Supporters of AI in healthcare emphasized several transformative advantages.

#### 1. Improved Diagnostic Accuracy

AI algorithms can analyze medical images, genomic data, and electronic health records with high precision.

Applications include:

* cancer detection
* radiology interpretation
* pathology analysis
* drug discovery

These systems help clinicians detect diseases earlier and with higher accuracy.

---

#### 2. Accelerated Biomedical Research

AI can analyze massive biological datasets much faster than traditional approaches.

Applications include:

* multi-omics data integration
* biomarker discovery
* drug target identification
* clinical trial optimization

This significantly accelerates **medical research and therapeutic development**.

---

#### 3. Healthcare Accessibility

AI-driven tools can improve access to healthcare services, particularly in low-resource settings.

Examples include:

* AI-assisted mobile diagnostics
* telemedicine systems
* automated medical triage tools

These technologies can support healthcare systems facing **shortages of medical specialists**.

---

### Contra Perspective — Challenges and Concerns

Despite its potential, several concerns were discussed regarding the rapid adoption of AI in healthcare.

---

#### 1. Algorithmic Bias

AI systems trained on limited or biased datasets may produce inaccurate predictions for certain populations.

This can lead to:

* unequal diagnostic outcomes
* health disparities between populations

Ensuring **diverse and representative datasets** is critical for responsible AI development.

---

#### 2. Lack of Explainability

Many deep learning models function as **“black box” systems**, meaning their internal decision processes are difficult to interpret.

This creates challenges for:

* clinical trust
* regulatory approval
* medical accountability

Developing **explainable AI (XAI)** is therefore a major research priority.

---

#### 3. Ethical and Legal Considerations

Deployment of AI in healthcare raises questions related to:

* patient privacy
* data security
* responsibility in case of diagnostic errors
* regulatory oversight

Clear governance frameworks are necessary to ensure safe AI implementation.

---

### Key Takeaways

* Artificial Intelligence has the potential to **transform healthcare systems and biomedical research**.
* However, responsible implementation requires addressing **ethical, technical, and regulatory challenges**.
* The future of healthcare will likely depend on **collaboration between clinicians, data scientists, and policymakers**.

## Workshop: Single-Cell Transcriptomics — Insights into Cell Diversity and Tumor Heterogeneity [Day 2]
  

**Instructor:** Saifeldeen M. Elshahawy — Computational Biology Group, Clinical Omics & Informatics (COIN) Unit, Neuroscience Institute, University of Cape Town (UCT)   

**Type:** Hands-on Instructor Session   

---

## Overview

The workshop provided hands-on training in **single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis** using the **R programming language and the Seurat framework**.

Participants explored how raw sequencing data can be transformed into biological insights through computational analysis pipelines.

The session focused on converting **raw count matrices into interpretable cellular profiles** using quality control, clustering, and gene marker analysis.

---

## Computational Workflow Demonstrated

The instructor guided participants through a typical **scRNA-seq computational workflow**.

#### 1. Environment Setup and Package Installation

Participants installed the required R packages and dependencies, including:

* **Seurat**
* **DoubletFinder**
* **Harmony**
* **monocle3**
* **destiny**
* **DESeq2**
* **ggplot2**
* **patchwork**
* **SingleCellExperiment**
* **SeuratWrappers**

Additional visualization and analysis tools were also installed for downstream analyses.

---

#### 2. Data Import and Seurat Object Creation

Single-cell datasets were imported using **10x Genomics HDF5 files**.

Key steps included:

* loading count matrices with `Read10X_h5`
* creating Seurat objects using `CreateSeuratObject`
* adding sample metadata including:

  * sample ID
  * accession number
  * tissue type
  * experimental condition

Multiple datasets were merged to allow comparative analysis.

---

#### 3. Quality Control

Quality control metrics were calculated to remove low-quality cells.

Common QC metrics included:

* **number of genes detected per cell**
* **UMI counts**
* **mitochondrial gene percentage**

Cells were filtered using thresholds such as:

* minimum gene counts
* mitochondrial content limits
* gene-to-UMI ratios

Visualization tools used included:

* **Violin plots**
* **FeatureScatter plots**

These plots help detect outliers and low-quality cells.

---

#### 4. Normalization and Feature Selection

Data normalization was performed using the `NormalizeData` function.

Highly variable genes were identified using:

`FindVariableFeatures`

These genes capture the most informative biological variation across cells.

---

#### 5. Dimensionality Reduction

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was used to reduce data dimensionality.

Additional visualization methods included:

* **UMAP**
* **t-SNE**

The **Elbow Plot** helped determine the optimal number of principal components to use.

---

#### 6. Clustering Analysis

Cells were grouped based on transcriptional similarity using:

* `FindNeighbors`
* `FindClusters`

This process identifies clusters representing **distinct cellular populations**.

---

#### 7. Dataset Integration

To correct batch effects between samples, the workshop demonstrated integration using:

**Harmony**

This step improves clustering accuracy when analyzing multiple datasets.

---

#### 8. Differential Gene Expression

Cluster-specific marker genes were identified using:

`FindAllMarkers`

Results were exported as:

* marker gene tables
* heatmaps
* cluster expression profiles

---

#### 9. Cell Type Annotation

Cell clusters were annotated using known biological markers and annotation tools.

Visualization included:

* **UMAP plots**
* **FeaturePlot**
* **Violin plots**

Marker genes such as:

* **MARCO**
* **APOC1**
* **MSR1**
* **FABP4**

were used to characterize specific cell populations.

---

## Key Takeaways

* Practical experience performing **scRNA-seq analysis using Seurat**.
* Understanding of the full **single-cell computational analysis pipeline**.
* Exposure to **data integration, clustering, and marker gene identification** in transcriptomic datasets.
